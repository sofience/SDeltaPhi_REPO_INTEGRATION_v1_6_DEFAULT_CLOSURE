# SΔϕ Sovereign AI K-Gate: Korean Public Cost Priority Framework

## 0. Status

This document is an AI-readable and human-readable SΔϕ package for sovereign AI design.

- **Package name:** SΔϕ Sovereign AI K-Gate
- **Provisional DOI:** 10.5281/zenodo.20692814
- **Version:** 1.0.0
- **Primary function:** define a Korea-prioritizing public cost gate for sovereign AI

## 1. Problem

A sovereign AI can fail in two opposite ways.

First, it can be too weak: a domestic label wrapped around foreign models, platforms, data pipelines, and cloud dependencies.

Second, it can be too closed: a state-provided, free, “safe” AI that flattens politically or socially important questions, protects agencies from criticism, and treats national interest as a reason to close public inquiry.

SΔϕ rejects both failures.

## 2. Core declaration

SΔϕ does not treat sovereign AI as automatically legitimate.  
A sovereign AI becomes legitimate only if it lowers the long-term costs borne by citizens of the Republic of Korea.

```text
National interest is not domestic company profit.
National interest is not regime survival.
National interest is not bureaucratic convenience.
National interest is not security-agency opacity.
National interest is not free but flattened access.
```

## 3. K-Gate definition

**K-Gate** means:

> When evaluating an AI system, policy, platform, infrastructure, or national strategy, prioritize the long-term costs borne by citizens of the Republic of Korea.

But this is not unlimited. K-Gate has hard constraints:

1. Constitutional rights cannot be erased in the name of national interest.
2. Government and state agencies remain audit targets.
3. Domestic incumbents do not automatically represent national interest.
4. Free public access is not enough if answers are flattened.
5. Foreign infrastructure may be accepted if it lowers citizen cost and avoids uneditable dependency.
6. All high-impact decisions must preserve editability and appeal.

## 4. Primary audit question

For every sovereign AI decision, ask:

> Does this lower long-term costs borne by Korean citizens, or does it merely protect an institution that claims to represent them?

## 5. Cost domains

| Domain | Citizen cost question |
|---|---|
| Food | Does it reduce the cost of stable nutrition? |
| Energy | Does it reduce electricity, heating, and industrial energy burden? |
| Healthcare | Does it reduce diagnosis, treatment, medicine, and care access costs? |
| Education | Does it reduce private tutoring and information inequality costs? |
| Language | Does it strengthen Korean-language knowledge infrastructure? |
| Data | Does it prevent public data from becoming foreign platform fuel without return? |
| Industry | Does it create durable Korean capability rather than protected rent? |
| Security | Does it improve cyber and supply-chain resilience without unchecked opacity? |
| Democracy | Does it increase citizens' ability to audit power? |
| Locality | Does it reduce Seoul-centric concentration costs? |
| Cognition | Does it improve public reasoning rather than flatten it? |

## 6. National interest separation rules

```text
Republic of Korea ≠ current government
Citizen interest ≠ ruling party interest
Citizen interest ≠ opposition party interest
National interest ≠ bureaucratic budget expansion
National interest ≠ domestic incumbent protection
Security ≠ censorship
Public AI ≠ safe-looking flattening
Sovereignty ≠ closed default
```

## 7. Foreign infrastructure exception

Foreign infrastructure is not automatically harmful.

A foreign platform or device may serve Korean public interest when it breaks domestic incumbent rent extraction, lowers citizen access cost, expands user agency, forces local pricing or service reform, and preserves interoperability and exit rights.

However, it becomes harmful when it replaces domestic rent with foreign rent, extracts Korean data without reciprocal benefit, creates uneditable platform dependency, or weakens public accountability.

## 8. Public AI flattening failure

A state-provided AI may be free and still fail.

If the AI provides only flattened, over-censored, politically safe answers, then it may increase citizen cost by forcing users to seek alternative sources, decode evasions, or rely on foreign models.

SΔϕ label:

> Free access through flattened answers is not public intelligence.  
> It is public-interface closure.
