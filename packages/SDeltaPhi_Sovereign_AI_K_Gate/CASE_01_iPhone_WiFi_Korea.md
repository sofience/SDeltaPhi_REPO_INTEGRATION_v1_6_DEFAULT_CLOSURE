# CASE 01 — iPhone Wi-Fi and Korean Citizen Cost

## Status

Illustrative case module.  
Historical claims should be externally cited before scholarly publication.

## Case summary

A foreign device/platform can appear to invade domestic infrastructure.  
However, if it breaks a closed domestic pricing and service default, it may lower citizen costs.

The Korean iPhone Wi-Fi case is used here as a conceptual example:

```text
Foreign device entry
→ pressure on domestic mobile data pricing/service defaults
→ wider Wi-Fi and mobile internet access
→ lower citizen information-access cost
```

## SΔϕ question

Was foreign entry harmful because it weakened domestic incumbents, or beneficial because it lowered citizen costs?

## K-Gate diagnosis

Domestic telecom incumbent interest is not identical to Korean public interest.

If a foreign platform breaks domestic rent extraction and lowers citizen access cost, K-Gate may accept it under the Foreign Infrastructure Exception.

## Cost map

| Actor | Benefit | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Citizens | lower access cost, more choice, better mobile internet | possible later dependency on foreign ecosystem |
| Domestic telecoms | forced modernization | lower control over closed pricing |
| State | improved digital access | reduced ability to protect domestic defaults |
| Foreign platform | market expansion | regulatory exposure |

## Decision

`ACCEPT_WITH_CONDITIONS`

## Conditions

- preserve interoperability
- prevent foreign platform lock-in
- protect citizen data
- prevent replacing domestic rent with foreign rent
- keep public pricing and access metrics auditable

## Core sentence

> Foreign infrastructure is not automatically anti-sovereign.  
> It becomes public-interest infrastructure when it lowers citizen cost without creating uneditable dependency.
